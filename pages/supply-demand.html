Supply & Demand

Identify the true origin of major price moves by finding areas where institutional orders outbalance the market.

What is Supply and Demand?

Supply and Demand are considered fundamental concepts in microeconomics. Put simply, it is the study of the interaction between buyers and sellers to identify the market price (or equilibrium price) and the quantities of the goods or services supplied.

Supply

Supply describes the total amount of a particular good or service available for purchase at a specific price. When the price rises, producers will manufacture more to cater to buyers' needs and increase revenue. By contrast, when the price falls, they tend to narrow production due to the inability to cover production costs. Therefore, there is a positive correlation between the price and the number of goods or services provided. The higher the price is, the bigger the quantity is, and vice versa.

Demand

Demand indicates the number of goods or services that buyers are willing to purchase at a certain price. Understandably, when a product is becoming costly, people tend to look for an alternative product or wait for its price to fall to a more reasonable price level for a buy decision. This causes the demand to drop. Otherwise, when the price of a product drops, buyers tend to believe this is a bargain and will be more interested in buying the product. To put it simply, the correlation between prices and demand is negative.

Equilibrium Price: In any market, the equilibrium price is the price where the demand quantity equals the supply quantity. As long as there is enough goods and service to satisfy buyers’ needs, the price may not fluctuate widely. Once one side exceeds the other in quantity, this will result in an imbalance. This imbalance will cause prices to fluctuate until it achieves a new balance.

Supply and Demand In Trading

In the financial market, the same principle applies. When the supply for an asset or instrument is high while the demand is low, this will drag the price lower. In contrast, when the demand exceeds supply, this will drive the price higher until a balance is achieved.

The constant interaction between buyers (aka "bulls") and sellers (aka "bears") results in the uptrend and downtrend in the market. Traders use the law of supply and demand in the financial market to make profits from market imbalances.

Demand Zones

In a demand zone, buyers exceed sellers. The price tends to visit demand zones on a retracement to match pending buy orders. After those orders are filled, the price will move on to find other demand areas where buyers exceed sellers.

Supply Zones

In a supply zone, sellers exceed buyers, driving the price lower according to the law of supply and demand. Generally, the supply zone, where many sell limit orders are waiting, is positioned above the current price level.

Market Balance – Imbalance In trading

The basic pattern of the market will go like this: balance → imbalance → balance → imbalance. When there is a difference between the supply quantity and demand quantity, an excess is established, and the market will trade in the direction of the predominant side until it reaches a balanced state again.

Balance Area

In a balanced market, the number of buyers and sellers is more or less even. This balance paves the way for a quick price consensus. In this type of market, the price often moves in a small range area (no need to make any substantial move to reach a fair level).

Imbalance Area

Once buyers or sellers gain control of the battle, the market switch to an imbalanced state where the price goes in the direction of the stronger player. If the bulls gain more advantage, the price will climb, otherwise, it will decline.

Trading with supply and demand entails a correct identification of a balanced zone, where we place our entry. Then, the following market imbalance will help traders to reap some profits. Professional traders are those who master the art of spotting a tradeable balanced area and patiently monitor the trade during the imbalance stage to make profits.

Different Patterns of Supply and Demand

Supply and demand areas can be found in two types of patterns on the chart: Reversal and Continuation.

Reversal Patterns

  • Drop-Base-Rally (DBR): Price is initially in a downtrend, forms a base, then rallies to the upside (Demand).
  • Rally-Base-Drop (RBD): Price starts with an uptrend, forms a base at the top, then falls (Supply).

Continuation Patterns

  • Drop-Base-Drop (DBD): Price drops, slows for a while on a base, then continues to fall (Supply).
  • Rally-Base-Rally (RBR): Price increases, slows for a while on a base, then continues to increase (Demand).

How To Draw Supply/Demand Zones

Drawing a zone is centered around determining two lines around a basing structure: the Proximal Line (nearer to current price) and the Distal Line (further from current price). The way you draw them depends on your risk tolerance.

  • Medium-Risk Method: Distal line at the extreme high/low of the base. Proximal line at the lowest/highest body price of the base.
  • High-Risk Method: Both lines placed at the extremes of the base range. Triggers earlier, but bigger stop loss.
  • Low-Risk Method: Both lines placed tightly around the highest/lowest body/prices. Better stop loss, but might miss the trade.

Zone Quality Enhancers (Scoring System)

Not all zones are tradable. Professional traders use a scoring system to filter out low-probability zones.

1. Strength of the move

Did the price leave the zone in a strong fashion with Extended-Range Candlesticks (ERCs)? (Stronger departure = higher score).

2. Time at the zone

A small number of candlesticks (1-3) shows a strong imbalance. More than 5 candles indicates a weak zone.

3. Freshness

Has the zone been tested before? A fresh, unvisited zone has the highest probability of holding.

4. Risk/Reward Ratio

The distance to the next opposing zone should offer at least a 1:3 Risk/Reward ratio.

Advanced Concepts

Flip Zones

A flip zone is an area where the price flips from support to resistance or vice versa. When a supply zone is broken, it can become a demand zone, and a broken demand zone can become a supply zone. Trading a retest of a flip zone can be highly profitable.

Multi-Timeframe (MTF) Analysis

MTF allows traders to grasp the big picture. You start by identifying the overall curve (the area between the nearest higher-timeframe supply and demand zones). You only look to sell high in the curve and buy low in the curve, using shorter timeframes for precise entries.
